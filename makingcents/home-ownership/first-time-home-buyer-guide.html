A Step-by-Step Guide to Buying Your First Home

Use this comprehensive guide to navigate the process of buying your first homefrom researching houses to signing on the dotted line.

Bottom Line Up Front

  • Evaluate your savings, credit score, income and long-term plans to decide if youre ready to buy your first home.
  • Research mortgage options, including special loans and programs for first-time buyers.
  • Take advantage of military-focused options such as VA loans with favorable terms and working with a Military Relocation Specialist.

Time to Read

12 minutes

December 24, 2024

Buying your first home is an exciting step toward establishing roots, building equity and achieving the American dream. Whats more, being a first-time homebuyer someone who hasnt owned a home in the past 3 yearsmeans you have a key advantage in the housing market. You may have access to special programs like no-down-payment loansFootnote [1] or favorable terms that can make homeownership more affordable. And, military members and Veterans may have even more advantages, including access to VA loans with competitive rates. 

But before you start house hunting or talking to mortgage lenders, its important to make sure youre ready to start the home-buying process. Throughout this detailed guide, well review the following steps involved in buying your first home:

  • Step 1: Determine if youre ready to buy your first home
  • Step 2: Figure out what you can afford
  • Step 3: Think about whats important to you
  • Step 4: Decide which type of mortgage loan you want
  • Step 5: Get mortgage loan quotes from several lenders
  • Step 6: Get preapproved for a mortgage
  • Step 7: Find a real estate agent
  • Step 8: Research homes within your budget
  • Step 9: Make an offer on the house you want
  • Step 10: Secure your mortgage loan
  • Step 11: Get a home inspection
  • Step 12: Go to closing
  • Step 13: Get ready to move into your new home
  • First-time homebuyer tips for active duty servicemembers and Veterans

Step 1: Determine if youre ready to buy your first home

Before you start browsing home listings or exploring mortgage options, make sure youre in a strong position to transition to homeownership. Take time now to evaluate your situation so you can move forward with confidence when you find the best home for you. 

Here are several factors to consider.

How much money you have available

Your down payment is only one aspect of buying property. There are plenty of other upfront expenses that come with homeownership, so your savings should cover more than just your down payment. 

Some loans do offer low or no-down-payment optionsFootnote [1] (including VA loans), but you still should plan to save additional money for closing costs (3%-6% of loan amount), moving expenses, initial repairs, emergency funds and any needed furniture or appliances. 

That means your total savings goals might be substantially larger than your target home price depending on your loan type, location, condition of the home and your individual needs.

Your home-buying budget, income and expenses

Your budget needs to support all aspects of homeownership, not just your new mortgage payment. Consider your monthly military income (including Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) for servicemembers), job stability and current debts. Look ahead at future home-related expenses like property taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance and possible HOA fees. 

  • A good rule of thumb: Your total monthly home expensesyour monthly mortgage payment, property taxes, home insurance and any HOA feesshouldnt exceed 28% of your gross monthly income.

Your current credit score

A solid credit score may open doors to better mortgage rates and terms. Many lenders want you to have a score of at least 620 for conventional loans, with the best rates typically being offered to applicants with credit scores above 720.

To help boost your score and chances of getting a more favorable mortgage rate, focus on making on-time payments and keeping credit card balances low. A higher credit score could help save you money over the life of your loan.

Resources to strengthen your credit score

With a good credit score, you can be more likely to qualify for better interest rates, which can save you money over time. Use our credit-building tips and solutions to improve your score so youll be in good shape when its time to apply for a mortgage.

The current housing market

Your local housing market affects your buying power. Research current home prices, mortgage rates and competition from other buyers in your target location. If youre a servicemember, check out the market near your duty station so you can make informed decisions about your purchase.

How long you plan to live in your new home

If possible, consider staying at least 3 to 5 years in your new home so you can build equity and potentially offset buying costs. Military members should factor in Permanent Change of Station (PCS) timelines and evaluate whether keeping the home as a rental property makes sense. Think about your career goals and family plans, too.

Your readiness to maintain a home

Youll need to prepare to handle regular upkeep and repairs, which means committing time and money to maintaining your home. Military members should also plan for maintenance during deployments. If you need assistance with these tasks, family members or neighbors may be willing to pitch in. Alternatively, consider working with a property management service to help keep things running smoothly.

Step 2: Figure out what you can afford

After you evaluate your readiness to buy, determine a realistic price range for your home search. Figure out how much home can you afford by getting familiar with all the costs involved. This knowledge will help you shop with confidence and avoid financial surprises.

Here are some of the typical expenses associated with homeownership. Factor these costs into your total budget and how much home you can afford.

Down payment

Your down payment may vary based on loan type and your situation. Conventional loans typically require 3%-20% down, while VA loans for servicemembers often need no down payment. FHA loans start at 3.5% down, and first-time homebuyer programs can help with down payment costs.

Private mortgage insurance

Monthly mortgage insurance costs depend on your down payment amount. Conventional loans typically require private mortgage insurance (PMI) if you put down less than 20%. While VA loans dont require PMI, they do have a one-time funding fee (which can be waived for disabled Veterans). 

Property taxes and homeowners insurance

Property taxes and home insurance vary by location and property type. Research the typical tax rates in your target neighborhoods to get an idea of how much those bills might be. Larger homes, historic districts or flood zones might mean higher insurance premiums. These costs often get folded into your monthly mortgage payment.

Renovations or repairs

Your home repair budget should reflect the propertys condition. New homes might need minimal repairs, while fixer-uppers require a bigger investment. Plan for both immediate repairs and long-term maintenance by saving 1%-4% of your homes value for annual upkeep.

Additional expenses

Total housing expenses often include utilities, HOA fees (if applicable), regular maintenance and unexpected repairs. Build a comfortable buffer into your budget to handle all these costs while maintaining your lifestyle and savings goals.

From deciding how much home you could afford to estimating closing costs, our mortgage and payment calculators can help you make smart financial decisions. 

Step 3: Think about whats important to you

Buying your first home is a major investment. Before you start house hunting, take time to identify your must-haves and nice-to-haves. Prioritizing these items helps you focus your search and make better decisions when evaluating properties.

The homes location

Consider commute times to work, schools if you have children and proximity to shopping, restaurants and entertainment. Research neighborhood safety, future development plans and local tax rates. For military families, think about how easy it would be to rent out the home if you receive PCS orders.

The propertys size

Your space requirements should match both current and future needs. Beyond the number of bedrooms and bathrooms, think about storage space, parking, home office options and room to grow. Also, consider whether you need a 1-story home for accessibility, a finished basement for extra living space or a large yard for kids and pets.

Your desired lifestyle

Your lifestyle preferences influence the type of home thats right for you. Think about how much time you want to spend on home maintenance tasks, especially if military service means frequent travel or deployment. For instance, a single-family home may offer more privacy and require more maintenance while a townhome may provide the opposite.

Step 4: Decide which type of mortgage loan you want

Understanding your mortgage options helps you choose the loan that best fits your situation. Each type of mortgage has different loan requirements and benefits, especially for military members and first-time buyers.

  • Conventional loan. Traditional mortgages come in 15-year or 30-year terms with fixed or adjustable rates. Youll typically need a credit score of at least 620 and a down payment of 3%-20%.Footnote [2] Opt for a fixed-rate mortgage for predictable payments or an adjustable-rate mortgage if you plan to move or refinance within a few years.
  • VA loans. Available to servicemembers, Veterans or eligible spouses, VA loans come with no down payment,Footnote [1] competitive rates and no PMI requirement. Youll need a Certificate of Eligibility and a credit score typically above 620 to qualify. The VA funding fee may be rolled into your loan amount. Some veterans may be waived for the VA funding fee. For example, if you receive VA benefits for a service-related disability, the VA funding fee can be waived. Your Certificate of Eligibility can show your funding fee exempt status.
  • FHA loans. Federal Housing Administration loans may work well if you have a lower credit score or smaller down payment. These government-backed loans accept credit scores as low as 580 with 3.5% down, but they require mortgage insurance throughout the loan term.
  • Specialty loans. These loan options include USDA loans for rural areas (no down payment required) and renovation loans for fixer-uppers that let you finance both the purchase and repairs. While only applicable in specific circumstances, they can be highly beneficial for homes that qualify. 

Considering a VA loan?

If youre eligible for a VA loan, its important to understand how it works before you apply. Check out our short video about the VA loan process. 

Step 5: Get mortgage loan quotes from several lenders

Shopping around for a mortgage helps you find the best available rates and terms. Most lenders expect you to compare offers, so getting multiple quotes within a 14-day period counts as only one credit inquiry.

Its sometimes helpful to seek out mortgage loan quotes from at least 3 different lenders so you can compare offers effectively. Request quotes on the same day since mortgage rates can change daily. 

Look at each lenders interest rate, APR, estimated closing costs, lender fees and points. For VA loans, find out if the lender has experience with military borrowers. Compare quotes and make sure youre considering all expected costs (not just the percentage of the interest rate).

Step 6: Get preapproved for a mortgage

Getting preapproved shows sellers youre a serious buyer. A preapproval letter can give you a clear budget and can help your offer stand out in a competitive market. 

The mortgage preapproval process involves a thorough review of your finances. Youll need to present recent pay stubs, 2 years of W-2s and tax returns and bank statements to your lender. Military members should include their Certificate of Eligibility for VA loans and any BAH documentation. Self-employed buyers typically need 2 years of business tax returns. 

After receiving documentation, lenders will check your credit, verify your income and employment and calculate your debt-to-income ratio. If everything checks out, theyll issue you a preapproval letter that you can present to sellers when submitting an offer.

A preapproval represents the maximum amount you can borrow when applying for a mortgage. Consider getting preapproved for less than your maximum so you can keep your monthly payments more comfortable. That may leave room in your budget for other homeownership costs.

Smart money tip

Most preapproval letters are valid for 60-90 days. During this time, avoid making major purchases or applying for new credit.

Step 7: Find a real estate agent

Working with the right real estate agent makes your home-buying journey smoother. A good agent serves as your guide, negotiator and local market expert. Look for someone who has experience helping first-time homebuyers, a strong knowledge of your target neighborhoods and a communication style that fits yours. Military families should talk with a Military Relocation Specialist who understands VA loans, PCS timelines and military-specific needs.

Your agent relationship works best with clear expectations, so talk to a few agents before choosing which one youll work with. Your agent interview questions should cover their experience, availability and processes. Request references from recent first-time buyers theyve helped and ask military families about their experience if youre an active-duty buyer.

Step 8: Research homes within your budget

When searching for a home, its important to think about your must-haves. Create a checklist of non-negotiable features (like number of bedrooms) versus nice-to-have amenities (like a finished basement).

Be sure to account for your financial limits, too. You can use your preapproval amount to guide your maximum home price. Search for homes at that threshold and below it to leave room for negotiations. 

A good house-hunting strategy should entail both online and in-person processes. Use real estate websites and apps to preview homes and neighborhoods. Your agent can also set up alerts for new listings and access off-market properties. 

Photos dont always paint a clear picture, so be sure to visit any properties youre seriously considering. For military buyers relocating from out of state, virtual tours and video walkthroughs can help you narrow down options before an in-person visit.

Buying a new home is more than just investing in property. Youre searching for the best place to build your life. Research the areas property values and any future development plans. Consider lifestyle factors like school quality, commute times and nearby amenities. 

Step 9: Make an offer on the house you want

When you find the right home, making a strong offer helps you stand out to sellers. Your real estate agent will guide you through this crucial step.

Your offer package should include the following key components:

  • the purchase price
  • your earnest money amount, which is typically 1%-3% of the offer price
  • your desired closing date
  • any contingencies that protect your interests

Some common contingencies may include:

  • a home inspection to verify the propertys condition
  • an appraisal to ensure the homes value matches the sale price
  • financing to protect your earnest money if the loan falls through
  • a military clause that addresses potential PCS orders (for military members)

In competitive markets, your offer strategy might also include a personal letter to the seller. Active duty military, Veterans and their families can share their service story. Many sellers appreciate the opportunity to help servicemembers find a home. Your agent should be able to advise whether this approach works well in your housing market.

Step 10: Secure your mortgage loan

Once your offer is accepted, its time to officially apply for a mortgage. The mortgage application process builds on your preapproval. Your lender will request updated versions of financial documents, including recent pay stubs and bank statements. For VA loans, your Certificate of Eligibility and DD-214 (or statement of service for active duty) must be current. To avoid mortgage approval processing delays, be sure to respond quickly to all requests for documentation.

A typical loan processing timeline usually takes 30-45 days. Heres what typically happens during this time frame:

  1. The lender orders a home appraisal.
  2. Underwriters review your complete financial profile.
  3. A title search confirms the propertys ownership history.
  4. You receive and review your loan estimate.
  5. Your interest rate can be locked in.

Your chief responsibility during processing is to avoid any major financial changes. Keep documentation of any large deposits to your accounts. Stay in close contact with your loan officer about any questions or concerns that arise.

Step 11: Get a home inspection

A professional home inspection helps you understand the property youre purchasing. This important step can save you from expensive surprises down the road. Make sure youre working with a licensed home inspector who can provide a complete review of the condition of the property. 

Items that should be inspected include:

  • foundation and structural elements
  • roof condition and expected lifespan
  • electrical and plumbing systems
  • HVAC system efficiency and age
  • signs of water damage or mold
  • insulation and ventilation
  • major appliances that convey (are included in the sale)

If something pops up on the inspection report, you have a few options. You can request repairs (curing) from the seller before closing. You could also attempt to negotiate a lower selling price to cover any needed work. 

If the inspection uncovers a serious problem, you could choose to walk away from the sale altogether. If you have an inspection contingency in your contract, it may protect you in this situation.

Step 12: Go to closing

Going to closing, or settlement, is the time when you sign all the home-buying paperwork and make the deal official. A few days before your closing date, youll receive your closing disclosure to review. Youll also want to complete a final walkthrough 24 hours before closing to verify the current condition of the home and see any repairs that were completed. 

Closing day transforms you from homebuyer to homeowner. Plan to spend about 2 hours reviewing and signing closing paperwork and paying your closing costs (bring a certified check or wire transfer confirmation). Then, youll receive the keys to your new home. 

Remember to bring your government-issued photo ID and proof of insurance with you to the closing. Some closings also offer electronic signing optionsif youre interested in this, ask your lender if its available in your situation.

Step 13: Get ready to move into your new home

A smooth move starts with good planning, especially if youre working within a military relocation timeline.

You should start working through your moving checklist about a month before moving day, which may take place before you sign your closing paperwork, depending on your contract details. 

Youll also need to decide whether youll hire a professional mover (military members, check your PCS moving benefits) and start gathering packing materials. 

Schedule to have your new utility services connected the day before your move, if possible. Also, update your address with the services youll continue using after the move, including:

  • banks, credit unions and credit card companies
  • insurance providers
  • vehicle registration and drivers license
  • employer and payroll
  • subscription services
  • USPS for mail forwarding

Before the moving trucks arrive at your new home, tackle basic home preparation tasks. For example, you can change locks, clean up and check smoke detectors. If possible, schedule painters or carpet cleaners to work in the house before you move in your belongings.

First-time homebuyer tips for active duty servicemembers and Veterans

As an active duty servicemember or Veteran, you have unique opportunities and considerations to make your military status work for you in your home-buying journey. Here are some of the ways you can use your benefits:

  • Explore VA loan benefits, including no down payment, no private mortgage insurance (PMI), competitive interest rates and a funding fee that can be rolled into your loan (or waived for disabled Veterans)
  • Add your Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) to your income to help boost how much you can borrow for a mortgage loan
  • Connect with an experienced Military Relocation Specialist
  • Include a military clause that addresses potential PCS orders in any contracts you put down
  • Evaluate a propertys rental potential, which you might use in the event of future PCS orders
  • Consider including a personal letter with your offer to share your service story with the sellers
  • Check your benefits for any PCS moving assistance thats available
